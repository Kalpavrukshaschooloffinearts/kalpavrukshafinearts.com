About Bharatanatyam

In depth

The name Bharatanatyam is a Sanskrit acronym:

✦ Bha — Bhava (expression, emotion)
✦ Ra — Raga (melody, music)
✦ Ta — Tala (rhythm, beat)
✦ Natyam — dance, drama, performance

The name also honours Bharata Muni, the ancient sage who authored the Natyashastra — the foundational treatise on classical performing arts (approx. 200 BCE–200 CE). Bharatanatyam is one of India’s eight classical dance forms, rooted in the Devadasi tradition of Tamil Nadu.

A complete Bharatanatyam journey — from first class to Arangetram — typically spans 6–10 years of regular training:

Beginner stage (1–2 years): Adavus, mudras, Alarippu, Jatiswaram, Salangai Pooja.
Intermediate stage (2–3 years): Varnam, Padam, Javali, Thillana, deep abhinaya.
Arangetram preparation (1–2 years): Intensive full-margam training and rehearsals.

Most students who begin at age 4–5 reach their Arangetram in their early teens. Adults starting at 25–30 can reach a high level in 5–7 years. Consistency — attending 2 sessions per week and practising at home — is the single biggest factor in pace of progress. At Kalpavruksha, progression is never rushed; Guru Deepa assesses each student individually.

Bharatanatyam has several distinct schools, each with a different lineage and aesthetic emphasis:

✦ Kalakshetra bani — devotional, sculptural, internationally recognised
✦ Pandanallur bani — strong geometric footwork, relatively minimal abhinaya
✦ Vazhuvoor bani — lyrical and flowing, Tamil folk influences
✦ Mysore / Thanjavur tradition — elaborate abhinaya rooted in the Bhakti court tradition

Kalpavruksha teaches exclusively in the Kalakshetra bani, widely regarded as the most academically rigorous and devotionally pure form of the art.

The Kalakshetra bani is a classical school of Bharatanatyam established by Rukmini Devi Arundale at the Kalakshetra Foundation in Chennai in 1936. Rukmini Devi is credited with restoring Bharatanatyam’s dignity as a classical art form after it had been marginalised during the colonial era.

The Kalakshetra style is defined by:
✦ Devotional purity — movements offered as a spiritual act
✦ Sculptural precision — each posture mirrors classical temple sculpture
✦ Restrained abhinaya — emotional expression is refined, not theatrical
✦ Natyashastra adherence — every element follows the ancient classical treatise

It is one of the most internationally respected and academically documented Bharatanatyam styles. Guru Smt. Deepa Sadasivam is a Kalakshetra Foundation alumna and teaches exclusively in this tradition.

Salangai Pooja & Arangetram

The Salangai Pooja is the sacred ceremony in which the student’s ankle bells are formally tied for the first time by the Guru — a deeply spiritual milestone marking their initiation as a classical dancer. It involves a public performance of approximately 1–1.5 hours and typically takes place after 1–2 years of training. Kalpavruksha has guided over 30 Salangai Poojas since 2014. Learn more →

The Arangetram (meaning “ascending the stage”) is a student’s first full-length classical solo performance — typically 2.5–3 hours — marking the transition from student to independent artist. It presents the complete margam (Pushpanjali, Alarippu, Jatiswaram, Shabdam, Varnam, Padams, Thillana, Mangalam) with a live orchestra. Learn more →

Typically 4–6 years of consistent training. The Salangai Pooja must be completed first. Readiness is assessed by Guru Deepa alone — based on technical mastery, abhinaya depth, and overall artistic maturity.
