About Bharatanatyam

In depth 9 questions

The name Bharatanatyam is a Sanskrit acronym:

• Bha — Bhava (expression, emotion)
• Ra — Raga (melody, music)
• Ta — Tala (rhythm, beat)
• Natyam — dance, drama, performance

The name also honours Bharata Muni, the ancient sage who authored the Natyashastra — the foundational treatise on classical performing arts (approx. 200 BCE–200 CE). Bharatanatyam is one of India's eight classical dance forms, rooted in the Devadasi tradition of Tamil Nadu and revived as a concert form in the 20th century.

A complete Bharatanatyam journey — from the very first class through to Arangetram (solo debut) — typically spans 6–10 years of regular training:

• Beginner stage: 1–2 years — adavus, mudras, Alarippu, Jatiswaram, Salangai Pooja
• Intermediate stage: 2–3 years — Varnam, Padam, Javali, Thillana, deep abhinaya
• Arangetram preparation: 1–2 years of intensive full-margam training

Most students who begin at age 4–5 reach their Arangetram in their early teens. Consistency — attending 2 classes per week and practising at home — is the single biggest factor in pace of progress. At Kalpavruksha, Guru Deepa assesses each student individually and advances them only when they are genuinely ready.

Bharatanatyam has several distinct schools or banis, each with a different lineage and aesthetic emphasis:

• Kalakshetra bani — refined, devotional, sculptural, internationally recognised
• Pandanallur bani — strong geometric footwork (nritta), relatively minimal abhinaya
• Vazhuvoor bani — lyrical, flowing, Tamil folk-influenced, expressive
• Mysore/Thanjavur tradition — elaborate abhinaya rooted in the Bhakti court tradition

Kalpavruksha teaches exclusively in the Kalakshetra bani, widely regarded as the most academically rigorous and devotionally pure form of the art.

The Kalakshetra bani is a classical school of Bharatanatyam established by Rukmini Devi Arundale at the Kalakshetra Foundation, Chennai, in 1936. Rukmini Devi is credited with restoring Bharatanatyam's dignity as a classical art form after it had been marginalised during the colonial era.

The Kalakshetra style is defined by: devotional purity — movements offered as a spiritual act; sculptural precision — each posture mirrors temple sculpture; restrained abhinaya — controlled, refined emotional expression; and strict adherence to the Natyashastra. It is considered one of the most internationally respected and academically documented Bharatanatyam traditions.

Guru Smt. Deepa Sadasivam of Kalpavruksha is a Kalakshetra Foundation alumna and teaches exclusively in this tradition.

Salangai Pooja & Arangetram

4 questions

The Salangai Pooja is the sacred ceremony in which the student's ankle bells (salangai) are formally tied for the first time by the Guru — a deeply spiritual milestone marking the student's initiation as a classical dancer. It involves a public performance of approximately 1–1.5 hours.

It typically takes place after 1–2 years of training, when Guru Deepa determines the student is ready. Kalpavruksha has guided over 20 Salangai Poojas since 2014. Learn more →

The Arangetram (arangu + etram, meaning "ascending the stage") is a student's first full-length classical solo performance, typically 2.5–3 hours — the transition from student to independent artist. It is the pinnacle milestone of a Bharatanatyam journey.

The performance presents the complete margam — Pushpanjali, Alarippu, Jatiswaram, Shabdam, Varnam, Padams, Thillana, and Mangalam — with a live orchestra. Learn more →

Typically 4–6 years of consistent training. The Salangai Pooja must be completed first. Readiness is assessed by Guru Deepa alone — based on technical mastery, abhinaya depth, stamina, and overall artistic maturity. The Arangetram preparation phase itself is 1–2 years of intensive training.

Yes — completely. We provide support across all aspects: dance training, music coordination, orchestra guidance, costume planning, ceremonial protocols, and rehearsals. We have guided 4+ Arangetrams at Kalpavruksha. Contact us to discuss your plans →
