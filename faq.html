🎊Salangai Pooja & Arangetram

The Salangai Pooja is the sacred ceremony in which the student's ankle bells (salangai) are formally tied for the very first time by the Guru — a deeply spiritual and emotional milestone marking the student's initiation as a classical dancer.

It involves a public performance of approximately 1–1.5 hours and typically takes place after 1–2 years of training, when Guru Deepa determines the student is ready. Kalpavruksha has guided over 20 Salangai Poojas since 2014. Learn more →

The Arangetram (arangu + etram, meaning "ascending the stage") is a student's first full-length classical solo performance, typically 2.5–3 hours, marking the transition from student to independent artist. It is the pinnacle milestone of a Bharatanatyam journey.

The performance presents the complete margam — Pushpanjali, Alarippu, Jatiswaram, Shabdam, Varnam, Padams, Thillana, and Mangalam — accompanied by a live orchestra. Learn more →

Typically 4–6 years of consistent training. The Salangai Pooja must be completed first. Readiness is assessed by Guru Deepa alone — based on technical mastery, abhinaya depth, stamina, and overall artistic maturity. The Arangetram preparation phase itself is 1–2 years of intensive training.

🌿About Bharatanatyam In-depth

A complete Bharatanatyam journey — from the very first class through to Arangetram (solo debut) — typically spans 6–10 years of regular training. Here's how the path breaks down:

  • Beginner stage: 1–2 years — adavus, mudras, basic compositions, Salangai Pooja
  • Intermediate stage: 2–3 years — Varnam, Padam, Javali, Thillana, deep abhinaya
  • Arangetram preparation: 1–2 years of intensive full-margam training

Most students who begin at age 4–5 reach their Arangetram in their early teens. Adults who start at 25–30 can reach a high level of proficiency in 5–7 years, though Arangetram timelines vary widely by individual. Consistency — attending 2 classes per week and practising daily at home — is the single biggest factor in how quickly a student progresses.

At Kalpavruksha, progression is never rushed. Guru Smt. Deepa Sadasivam assesses each student individually and advances them only when they are genuinely ready — technically, artistically, and emotionally.

The Kalakshetra bani is a classical school of Bharatanatyam established by Rukmini Devi Arundale at the Kalakshetra Foundation in Chennai in 1936. Rukmini Devi is credited with restoring Bharatanatyam's dignity as a classical art form after it had been marginalised during the colonial era.

The Kalakshetra style is defined by:

  • Devotional purity — movements are offered as a spiritual act, not theatrical entertainment
  • Sculptural precision — each posture and line mirrors the sculptures of classical temple art
  • Restrained abhinaya — emotional expression is controlled and refined, not exaggerated
  • Strict adherence to the Natyashastra — every element of the dance follows the ancient classical treatise

It is considered one of the most internationally respected and academically documented Bharatanatyam styles. Guru Smt. Deepa Sadasivam of Kalpavruksha is a Kalakshetra Foundation alumna and teaches exclusively in this tradition.

Bharatanatyam has several distinct schools or banis, each rooted in a different lineage and emphasising different aesthetic qualities:

  • Kalakshetra bani (Rukmini Devi Arundale) — refined, devotional, sculptural, internationally recognised
  • Pandanallur bani — strong emphasis on geometric footwork (nritta) and precise body positions; relatively minimal abhinaya
  • Vazhuvoor bani — lyrical and flowing, with Tamil folk influences; expressive and accessible
  • Mysore/Thanjavur tradition — elaborate abhinaya rooted in the Bhakti court tradition; ornate and literary

Kalpavruksha teaches exclusively in the Kalakshetra bani, which is widely regarded as the most academically rigorous and devotionally pure form of the art.

Yes — Bharatanatyam is widely recognised as one of the most comprehensive classical disciplines for a child's development. Benefits include:

  • Physical: Improves posture, coordination, balance, and flexibility. Strengthens leg and core muscles through the Aramandi (half-sit) posture
  • Cognitive: Develops memory and concentration through the memorisation of complex adavu sequences, compositions, and rhythmic patterns
  • Emotional: Teaches children to express a full range of emotions through abhinaya (expressive storytelling), building emotional intelligence
  • Cultural: Connects children to classical Indian literature, mythology, music, and philosophy in a living, embodied way
  • Discipline: Regular structured training from a young age builds work ethic and commitment that carries into academic and professional life

Children who train consistently in Bharatanatyam typically develop a level of body awareness and artistic sensibility that enriches every area of their lives.

The name Bharatanatyam comes from the Sanskrit acronym:

  • Bha — Bhava (expression, emotion)
  • Ra — Raga (melody, music)
  • Ta — Tala (rhythm, beat)
  • Natyam — dance, drama, performance

The name is also a tribute to Bharata Muni, the ancient sage who authored the Natyashastra — the foundational treatise on classical performing arts, written approximately 200 BCE–200 CE. Bharatanatyam is one of India's eight classical dance forms and is rooted in the Devadasi tradition of Tamil Nadu, later revived and codified as a concert form in the 20th century.
